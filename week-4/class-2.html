Concept

Zero-Shot: Asking Directly

Zero-shot means you give the task with no examples of the desired output — you rely entirely on the instruction and the model's training.

  • Works well for common, well-defined tasks: translation, simple summarization, classification with obvious categories.
  • Fails when the task needs a specific format, style, or edge case the model can't infer from words alone.
  • Zero-shot is the fastest to write — always try it first, then upgrade if the output misses the mark.
  • Example: "Classify this review as positive, negative, or neutral" — clear enough with zero examples.

Concept

Few-Shot: Show, Don't Just Tell

Few-shot prompting gives the model 2–5 input/output examples before the real task — it learns the pattern, not just the rule.

  • Best for consistent formatting, unusual output shapes, tone-matching, and edge cases hard to describe in words.
  • The pattern in your examples is the instruction — inconsistent examples produce inconsistent output.
  • Two or three well-chosen examples usually beat ten mediocre ones.
  • Order can matter: models weight recent examples more — put your best, most representative example last.

  • Two examples were enough to teach the format: short, punchy, title case.
  • The model completed the pattern rather than being told rules about headline writing.

Concept

Assigning a Persona

System.
"You are a senior security engineer reviewing code for junior developers. Be direct, flag risks first, explain briefly."
User.
"Review this login function for issues."
  • Personas shift tone and framing, not facts — a persona doesn't make the model more accurate, just more consistent in voice.
  • Useful for brand voice, audience-appropriate explanations, and focusing attention (a "security engineer" flags risks first).
  • Don't rely on a persona alone for correctness — pair it with a clear task and, when needed, examples.

Concept

Chain-of-Thought: Reasoning Before Answering

Asking the model to reason step by step before giving a final answer often improves accuracy on multi-step problems.

  • Phrase it directly: "Think through this step by step, then give your final answer on the last line."
  • Helps most on arithmetic, logic, and multi-step reasoning — less useful for simple factual lookups.
  • Trade-off: longer, slower, costlier responses — and reasoning text can sound confident while being wrong.
  • For production, ask for reasoning and the final answer in clearly separated sections, or hide the reasoning from end users.

Concept

Choosing the Right Technique

→Zero-Shot

Simple, well-known task with an obvious answer shape.

→Few-Shot

A specific format or tone you need the model to match exactly.

→Role-Based

Consistent voice across many calls — set it once in the system prompt.

→Chain-of-Thought

Multi-step reasoning, arithmetic, or logic puzzles.

＋Combine Freely

A system persona, a few examples, and "think step by step" can all stack in one prompt — most real prompts use two or three techniques together.

Watch For This

Common Pitfalls

✕

Inconsistent ExamplesFew-shot examples that contradict each other confuse the pattern.

✕

Too Many ExamplesPadding the prompt with ten examples when three would do wastes tokens.

✕

Persona Mistaken for AccuracyA confident "expert" persona can still be wrong — verify facts independently.

✕

Order BiasA weak example placed last can drag down output quality — lead and close strong.

✕

Exposed ReasoningShipping raw chain-of-thought text to end users looks unpolished and can leak errors.

✕

Overfitting the FormatExamples so narrow the model can't generalize to a slightly different input.

Recap

What We Revised

  • Zero-shot is your fast default — upgrade only when the output falls short.
  • Few-shot teaches pattern and format through examples, not description.
  • Personas shape voice and framing, not factual accuracy.
  • Chain-of-thought helps multi-step reasoning at the cost of length and speed.
  • These techniques stack — most real prompts combine two or three.
