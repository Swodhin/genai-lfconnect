Concept 01

What LLMs Are Genuinely Good At

Before critiquing the failure modes, it's worth being precise about the real, repeatedly-verified strengths.

Drafting & Summarizing

Turning rough notes into readable prose, or condensing long documents into key points.

Brainstorming & Rewriting

Generating options, alternate phrasings, or tones for a given piece of text.

Code Assistance

Explaining, translating between languages, and drafting boilerplate or tests.

Translation & Style Transfer

Converting between languages or reading levels, given a source text to ground it.

  • Notice the pattern: these tasks give the model something concrete to transform — not something to invent from scratch and verify unaided.
Concept 02

Hallucination: Confident Nonsense

A hallucination is fluent, plausible-sounding output that is factually wrong or entirely invented — stated with the same confidence as a correct answer.

  • Because the model is optimized to produce plausible next tokens, not verified facts, a confident tone is never proof of accuracy.
  • Hallucinations are most common for obscure facts, exact numbers, quotes, and citations — anywhere plausible-sounding detail is easy to generate but hard to verify.
  • The model has no internal "I'm not sure" signal by default — it will complete a fabricated citation just as fluently as a real one.
prompt: "Cite the 2019 study where Dr. A. Renwick proved goldfish have a 3-second memory."

model: "In Renwick (2019), Journal of Aquatic Cognition, vol. 14, researchers found goldfish retain memories for only three seconds..."

reality: No such study, journal, or author exists — the claim is a well-known myth, and the model fabricated a citation to match it.
Concept 02, continued

Why Hallucination Happens

  • No built-in fact-checker: the model has no live database lookup by default — it generates the most plausible continuation, not the most verified one.
  • Pattern completion, not retrieval: asked for a citation, it produces something citation-shaped, whether or not the source is real.
  • Training data cutoff: the model's knowledge freezes at training time — anything after that, or rare enough to be underrepresented, is a gap it may paper over.
  • Pressure to answer: models are tuned to be helpful and give an answer, which can bias them toward guessing rather than declining.
Concept 03

Bias In, Bias Out

LLMs learn statistical patterns from real-world text — and real-world text carries the biases, stereotypes, and representation gaps of the societies that produced it.

Stereotyped associations

A model may default to gendered or culturally skewed assumptions in stories, job examples, or generated images unless prompted otherwise.

Representation gaps

Languages, regions, and topics with less training data are handled less reliably — quality is not evenly distributed.

Amplification at scale

A subtle bias repeated across millions of automated outputs can compound into a much larger real-world effect.

Mitigations exist, not cures

Fine-tuning, filtering, and instructions can reduce visible bias, but rarely eliminate it — vigilance is still required.

Concept 04

Other Failure Modes to Expect

Arithmetic & logic slips

Multi-step math or precise counting can go wrong even when the prose around it reads confidently.

Stale knowledge

Anything after the training cutoff — recent events, new releases — may be missing, guessed, or wrong.

Prompt injection

Untrusted text (a webpage, a document) can contain hidden instructions that hijack the model's behavior.

Run-to-run inconsistency

The same prompt can yield different answers across calls, especially at higher temperature.

Concept 05

Responsible Use, in Practice

None of these limitations mean "don't use LLMs" — they mean use them with the right safeguards.

01

Verify important facts — especially numbers, quotes, citations, and anything with real-world consequences.

02

Keep a human in the loop for consequential decisions — hiring, medical, legal, or financial output should be reviewed, not shipped blind.

03

Disclose AI involvement where it matters to your audience or organization's policy.

04

Protect sensitive data — don't paste confidential or personal information into prompts without checking data-handling policies.

05

Watch for skewed outputs and adjust prompts, review criteria, or tooling when you spot a pattern of bias.

Common Pitfalls

Misconceptions That Get People Burned

"If it sounds confident, it's correct"

Fluency and accuracy are produced by different mechanisms — confidence of tone tells you nothing about truth.

"AI output is neutral and objective"

Every model reflects choices in training data and tuning — there is no bias-free default.

"One better prompt fixes bias"

Prompting can reduce visible bias in a single response, but doesn't remove it from the underlying model.

"It will tell me when it doesn't know"

Models often guess fluently instead of declining — don't rely on self-reported uncertainty.

Key Takeaways

  • 01LLMs excel at transforming existing text — drafting, summarizing, rewriting, and code assistance.
  • 02Hallucination happens because models generate plausible text, not verified facts — confidence is not accuracy.
  • 03Bias, stale knowledge, and inconsistency are structural properties of how these models are trained, not occasional bugs.
