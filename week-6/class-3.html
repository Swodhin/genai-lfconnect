Why Chunk Documents?

  • Long documents don't fit neatly into an embedding or a prompt — we split them into smaller pieces called chunks.
  • A chunk is usually paragraph-sized: small enough to embed precisely, big enough to keep its meaning.
  • Chunk too small (a single sentence) and you lose the surrounding context.
  • Chunk too large (a whole chapter) and the embedding blurs many ideas into one vector, hurting retrieval precision.
  • Good chunking is one of the highest-leverage decisions in a RAG system — often more impactful than which LLM you pick.

Concept

Chunking Strategies & Trade-Offs

Fixed-Size

  • Split by a fixed count of characters or tokens
  • Simple and fast to implement
  • Usually overlaps neighboring chunks

Sentence / Paragraph

  • Splits along natural sentence or paragraph breaks
  • Keeps ideas whole and readable
  • A solid default for most prose

Semantic

  • Splits where the topic actually changes
  • Needs an extra pass comparing sentence embeddings
  • Most precise, more expensive to build

Overlap between neighboring chunks (10–20%) keeps an idea from being cut in half right at a boundary.

Concept

Constructing a Grounded Prompt

SYSTEM: Answer using ONLY the context below. If the answer isn't in the context, say you don't know. CONTEXT: {retrieved_chunk_1} {retrieved_chunk_2} QUESTION: {user_question}
  • Retrieved chunks are inserted as "context," right alongside the question.
  • Explicit instructions ("answer only from context") curb the model's urge to fall back on parametric guessing.
  • Telling it to admit uncertainty is often the single highest-value instruction in a RAG prompt.

A Working Mini Chatbot

Corpus (3 tiny documents)

Doc 1: "New hires accrue 15 paid vacation days per year, credited monthly."
Doc 2: "Submit expense reports within 30 days of purchase."
Doc 3: "Remote employees must be reachable 10am–3pm local time."

Question: "How many vacation days do new hires get?"

Retrieved: Doc 1 (highest cosine similarity)

Answer: "New hires get 15 paid vacation days per year, credited monthly — according to the vacation policy."

Limitations

Where a Simple RAG Chatbot Falls Short

Retrieval Quality Ceiling

Garbage retrieved means a garbage answer, no matter how good the LLM is.

No Built-In Citations

Without extra work, users can't verify exactly where an answer came from.

Stale Index

If source documents change and you don't re-embed, answers quietly go out of date.

Latency & Cost Stack Up

Embedding, retrieval, and generation each take time and money, every single question.

Where To Go From Here

Beyond the Simple Pipeline

  • Re-ranking: use a second, more precise model to reorder top candidates before generating.
  • Hybrid search: combine keyword and semantic search for the best of both.
  • Metadata filtering: narrow retrieval by date, author, or document type first.
  • Citations: return the source chunk alongside the answer so it can be verified.
  • Evaluation: measure retrieval precision and answer quality, not just "it looks right."
