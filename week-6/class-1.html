Concept

The Knowledge-Cutoff Problem

  • Every LLM is trained on a snapshot of text collected up to a certain date — its training cutoff.
  • After that date, the model has never seen a single new word about the world.
  • Ask it about last week's news, this morning's price, or your company's internal wiki — it has nothing to draw on.
  • It doesn't know that it doesn't know, and will often guess fluently and confidently anyway.
  • That confident guessing is hallucination: a plausible answer with no grounding in fact.
  • The fix isn't a smarter model — it's giving the model something real to read before it answers.

Concept

Parametric vs. Retrieved Knowledge

Parametric Knowledge

  • Baked into the model's weights during training
  • Broad, but frozen at the training cutoff
  • Can't be updated without expensive retraining
  • Prone to blending facts together — hallucination

Retrieved Knowledge

  • Lives in documents outside the model entirely
  • Updated any time — just edit a file
  • Specific to your data: your docs, your product
  • The model reads it fresh on every single request

Concept

Grounding: Give It Something to Read

  • Grounding means supplying real source text in the prompt so the model answers from what's in front of it, not from memory.
  • Simplest form: paste the relevant paragraph directly into your prompt, then ask the question.
  • This already works today — no vector database required for a handful of documents.
  • The catch: it doesn't scale. You can't paste ten thousand pages into every prompt.
  • That scaling problem is exactly what Retrieval-Augmented Generation automates.

Concept

RAG vs. Fine-Tuning

Retrieval-Augmented Generation

  • Adds an "open book" at question time
  • Updatable in seconds — swap a document
  • Can point back to its sources
  • Best for facts, policies, and documents

Fine-Tuning

  • Re-trains the model's own weights
  • Slow and costly to update
  • No built-in citations
  • Best for tone, style, and behavior

Many production systems use both: fine-tune for voice, RAG for facts.

From the Notebook

Ask It, Then Show It the File

No Context — Parametric Only

Prompt: "What is our office's guest Wi-Fi password policy?"
Model: invents a plausible-sounding policy that isn't real.

With Context — Grounded

Prompt: [pasted policy paragraph] + "What is our office's guest Wi-Fi password policy?"
Model: correctly quotes the real policy from the pasted text.

Same model, same question — the only thing that changed is what it was allowed to read.

Common Pitfalls

Misconceptions Worth Correcting

Not Live Internet Access

RAG only retrieves from documents you've actually given it — nothing more.

More Context Isn't Always Better

Irrelevant text dilutes the model's focus and can crowd out the right answer.

A Bad Retriever Looks Like a Bad Model

If retrieval finds the wrong chunk, generation fails too — even with a great model.

Garbage In, Garbage Out

Messy, outdated, or duplicated source documents poison every answer downstream.

Recap

Key Takeaways

  • LLMs only know what was in their training data — they have a knowledge cutoff.
  • Parametric knowledge is frozen; retrieved knowledge is fresh and updatable.
  • Grounding means handing the model real text before asking it a question.
  • RAG automates grounding at scale: chunk, embed, store, retrieve, generate.
  • RAG and fine-tuning solve different problems — facts vs. behavior.
